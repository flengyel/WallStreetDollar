The Wall Street Dollar

The currency for exposure to conflict of interest risk

Wall Street says it wants government to stay out of its business, but Wall Street really wants government to give it business. The Street depends on government to print its currency and set monetary policy. Since Wall Street cannot live with any regulation, despite privileged access to a sovereign currency, market logic dictates that Wall Street create its own currency: the Wall Street Dollar (WSD, symbol: W$).

The worth of Wall Street Dollars will be determined by the supply and demand for Wall Street's most advanced, exotic, highly mathematized products.

Proprietary trading, investment rating, financial executive compensation, and finance industry political contributions would be priced and remunerated in WSD, the medium of exchange for exposure to conflict of interest risk in the financial industry.

Currently, the Wall Street Dollar tracks atmospheric noise. If common sense prevails, a global 24/7 exchange will be created for Wall Street Dollars. Main Street will have the USD, Wall Street will have the WSD, and the Fed will slam the discount window shut on the shadow banking system for good.

Frequently Asked Questions

How will the Wall Street Dollar affect taxpayers?

Main Street would be relieved of the necessity to maintain US Dollar reserves for a systemic collapse of the financial markets. Taxpayers could buy Wall Street Dollars if the probability of a bank bailout is low, and short them if the probability of a bailout is high. Vanilla stocks and bonds, which don't make money for Wall Street, will continue to be traded in US Dollars. Comparison of stock and bond prices in Wall Street Dollars versus US Dollars would inform investors about their exposure to conflict of interest risk in these securities.

What does the Wall Street dollar mean for investment banks?

The Wall Street Dollar and the WSD exchange would provide a source of bank bailout funds and a new source of markets. Securities valuation would benefit through the explicit inclusion of conflict of interest risk in pricing models.

Why would anyone use Wall Street Dollars?

Financial institutions would be required to use the WSD for proprietary trading, investment rating, executive compensation, and political contributions. This would provide a market for Wall Street Dollars.

Who would guarantee the Wall Street Dollar?

The WSD will be backed by the full faith and credit of Wall Street--not the United States government. Wall Street could pay a credit ratings agency in WSD for a triple-A rating, and the price of WSD would reflect this.

What about bank holding company access to the Fed discount window?

Some investment banks were converted to bank holding companies so they could be given access to the Federal Reserve Bank discount window. This access would be phased out with the conversion to Wall Street Dollars.

Will the FDIC continue to insure the bonds of bailed out financial institutions?

The FDIC insured the bonds of bailed out financial institutions through programs such as the Temporary Liquidity Guarantee Program (TLGP). Without this guarantee, no one would purchase the debt of a bailed out institution. Special programs such as the TLGP, which are funded with US Dollars, would be phased out with the conversion to the Wall Street Dollar.

What if the Fed were to close the discount window to investment banks?

In the absence of a conversion to Wall Street Dollars, if the institutions converted to bank holding companies were denied access to the Fed discount window, and if the FDIC ceased insuring their bonds, their stocks would probably drop to zero within a week.

Why not just short the stock of a bank that is about to be bailed out?

Because it is more efficient to shift exposure to conflict of interest risk to a currency and exchange designed to contain and allocate it. Stock prices need not reflect conflict of interest risk: front-running would be pointless if they did. Shorting bank stocks will not provide bailout funds if banks need to be recapitalized.

Would securitizing conflict of interest risk institutionalize unethical behavior?

Insurance will promote risk taking in general, but the benefits outweigh the harms. Regulating financial markets is becoming prohibitively expensive. Creating a currency and an exchange to insure against negative externalities inherent in financial markets is a pragmatic approach to regulating them. Enabling conflict of interest risk to be priced explicitly and hedged against when needed will provide institutional incentives to encourage ethical behavior. Pricing executive compensation in Wall Street Dollars will ensure favorable exchange rates.

How would the WSD exchange deter speculation in US Dollars?

A number of proposals are under consideration. Cross-currency swaps could be prohibited. Random numbers could be used to introduce small unpredictable delays into the transaction processing of the WSD exchange to ensure fairness and to thwart high-frequency trading and front-running.
